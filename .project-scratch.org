Human action prediction is a common task in computer vision that aims to predict actions in video data. Where such predictions are required quickly and as part of a human responsive interaction loop, the problem becomes one of action anticipation, in which the aim is to accurately predict actions in as few observed frames as possible. Example applications include human-robot teaming [21], AR/VR applications [20], safety monitoring in video surveillance [15], among others where a timely response is critical to the overall system performance. The task of human action prediction focuses on the accuracy, speed and frame latency of action classification [8]. In such cases, rapid low-latency action anticipation may allow interactive systems to pre-load resources such as relevant objects in mixed reality (XR) systems, or pruning and/or priming potential response actions in human-machine interfacing contexts, supporting quicker, more natural interactions.
Anticipatory action prediction is well established in the primate brain. In particular, the role of early visual motion cues are well understood to offer predictive and response-priming cues during human movement and action [7]. In human vision, these motion cues are perceived at early stages of visual processing, made available from optical flow - the perceived movement of light patterns projecting onto the eye - to prime other centres of the brain (e.g., the pre-motor cortex) [13]. Human vision research indicates that the human motor system plays a crucial role when observing and predicting other’s actions [6]. In primate brains, kinematic information of human skeletal interest points, typically joints, supports human action prediction, as well as emotional intent understanding. This motivates consideration of how such kinematic information, as well as early visual motion cues, can be used to support similar capabilities of visual interpretation of human movements in computer vision scenarios.
Human skeleton keypoints are 2D or 3D locations of major body joints extracted from images or videos, providing a compact and interpretable representation of human pose in each frame. However, skeleton keypoints are inherently static descriptors and do not capture the local motion context surrounding each joint. Although tracking keypoints across frames yields displacement vectors, this point-based information omits important second-order motion characteristics—such as instantaneous accelerations, rotations, or subtle surface deformations—that are only evident in the surrounding optical flow field. As a result, skeleton-only representations encode joint positions and coarse movements but miss the richer local dynamics that are critical for early action understanding.
For actions with similar gross body movements—such as distinguishing a handshake from a punch—keypoint-only models are unlikely to make accurate predictions until most of the action sequence has unfolded. In contrast, keypoint-anchored visual motion cues (e.g., wrist acceleration, torso rotation or deformation) may provide early discriminative signals that support faster and more reliable action prediction.
Action classification and action anticipation are two separate but related tasks. Action classification, sometimes called offline action recognition, is retrospective recognition, where the full action has been completed and observed, and a label (or labels) are assigned. Action anticipation is the prediction of an a label (or labels) before the action has fully unfolded, using a partial observation of the full action [8, 3].
