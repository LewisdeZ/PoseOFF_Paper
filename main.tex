\section{Introduction}
% Why is human early action recognition important?
Human action prediction is a common task in computer vision that aims to predict actions in video data.
Where such predictions are required quickly and as part of a human responsive interaction loop, the problem becomes one of action anticipation, in which the aim is to accurately predict actions in as few observed frames as possible.
Example applications include human-robot teaming~\cite{yu2021MultisensorGestureInteractionSystemHumanrobotCooperation}, AR/VR applications~[CITE], safety monitoring in video surveillance~\cite{ryoo2011HumanactivitypredictionEarlyrecognitionongoingactivitiesstreamingvideos}, among others.
The task of human action prediction focuses on the accuracy, speed and frame latency of action classification~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey}.
In such cases, rapid low-latency action anticipation may allow interactive systems to pre-load resources such as pre-loading relevant objects in mixed reality (XR) systems, or pruning and/or priming potential response actions in human-machine interfacing contexts, supporting quicker, more natural interactions.

% Human early visual motion cues
Anticipatory action prediction is well established in the primate brain.
In particular, the role of early visual motion cues are well understood to offer predictive and response-priming cues during human movement and action [CITE].
In human vision, these motion cues are perceived at early stages of visual processing, made available from optical flow - the perceived movement of light patterns projecting onto the eye - to prime other centres of the brain (e.g., the pre-motor cortex)~\cite{meichow2023Eyemovementsopticflowperception}.

Human vision research indicates that the human motor system plays a crucial role when observing and predicting other's actions \cite{gredeback2015EyeMovementsActionObservation}.
In primate brains, kinematic information of human skeletal interest points, typically joints, supports human action prediction, as well as emotional intent understanding.

This motivates the consideration of how such kinematic information, as well as human early visual motion cues, can be used to support similar capabilities of visual interpretation of human movements in computer vision scenarios.

% Human skeleton based action recognition
Human skeleton keypoints are 2D or 3D locations of major body joints extracted from images or videos, providing a compact and interpretable representation of human pose in each frame.
However, skeleton keypoints are inherently static descriptors and do not capture the local motion context surrounding each joint.
Although tracking keypoints across frames yields displacement vectors, this point-based information omits important second-order motion characteristics—such as instantaneous accelerations, rotations, or subtle surface deformations—that are only evident in the surrounding optical flow field.
As a result, skeleton-only representations encode joint positions and coarse movements but miss the richer local dynamics that are critical for early action understanding.

For actions with similar gross body movements—such as distinguishing a handshake from a punch—keypoint-only models are unlikely to make accurate predictions until most of the action sequence has unfolded.
In contrast, keypoint-anchored visual motion cues (e.g., wrist acceleration, torso rotation or deformation) may provide early discriminative signals that support faster and more reliable action prediction.

% Action anticipation
Action classification and action anticipation are two separate but related tasks.
In action classification, sometimes called offline action recognition, is retrospective recognition, where the full action has been completed and observed, and a label (or labels) are assigned.
Action anticipation is the prediction of an a label (or labels) before the action has fully unfolded, using a partial observation of the full action.

We are not the first to consider optical flow for the purpose of action recognition [REFS].
While others have considered the role of optical flow, no one, to the best of our knowledge has considered it's use specifically for capturing localised visual motion petterns anchored at skeletal key points.
Most unique about this work is that we are defining local regions of flow so as to allow prediction models to not just exploit the tracked spatial patterns in each point-anchored flow field, but to learn the relationship with other point anchored patches.
Not simply predicting trajectories
Exploit spatial motion patterns present at the onset of actions, which may provide anticipatory benefit.

To address these challenges, we propose a method of embedding localised spatio-temporal features extracted from keypoint-anchored windows of optical flow in existing skeleton-based action recognition models.
Our Pose-guided Optical Flow Feature extraction method (PoseOFF) involves the strategic sampling of windows of optical flow surrounding human skeleton keypoints.
Optical flow can provide more nuanced information than the simple displacement vectors of skeleton keypoints.
A simple embedding layer integrated in three state-of-the-art skeleton-based action recognition algorithms shows an improvement in classification accuracy and action anticipation.

% Important recent methods
Action classification and anticipation are long standing problems in computer vision, with increasing interest in approaches that can support real-time and online action prediction.
In this work, we focus specifically on methods that employ skeleton keypoints for these tasks.
The nature of the 2D or 3D points that represent human skeleton keypoints lends itself well to graph-based learning approaches.
% Skeleton-action recognition...
State-of-the-art skeleton-based human action recognition algorithms, such as ST-GCN++~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition} and MS-G3D~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition} utilise Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) architecture to learn to classify human actions in spatio-temporal skeleton sequences.
These models are designed to perform human action classification; making a prediction after viewing the complete action sequence.


% Action anticipation
To approach the problem of human action anticipation using human skeleton keypoints, Chi et al.~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition} incorporated a Neural Ordinary Differential Equations module into a skeleton-based action recognition model to predict future skeleton states.
Their model, InfoGCN++, was able to classify human actions in skeleton datasets with fewer observed frames than existing skeleton-based action recognition models.

% Visual motion in action prediction
Skeleton-based approaches to human action classification and anticipation neglect local dynamics present in RGB videos that may provide discriminative signals about the nature of a human action before it's conclusion.
The efficacy of visual motion cues for early action prediction is supported by biological vision research.
% Biological vision examples of fast action prediction
In many ball-sports, it is nearly impossible for the human eye to accurately track the trajectory of the ball.
A meta-analysis~\cite{mann2007PerceptualCognitiveExpertiseSportMetaAnalysis} of eye-tracking research in sporting contexts indicates that elite athletes direct their gaze to information-dense areas.
Football goalkeepers that received perception training on areas of a penalty taker's body to focus on showed an increase performance when anticipating the direction of the penalty taker's shot~\cite{savelsbergh2010Anticipationpenaltykickingdirectioncanbeimproveddirectingattentionperceptuallearning}.
% In social situations, humans fixate primarily on the torso, and self report that their attention focuses on hands, elbows and feet~\cite{ennisEyetracktiveMeasuringattentionbodypartswhenjudginghumanmotions}.

% Flow for human action recognition and action anticipation
This motion information can be extracted and learned through optical flow.
Cai et al.~\cite{cai2019TemporalHockeyActionRecognitionPoseOpticalFlows} proposed a two-stream architecture for hockey action recognition that utilised both optical flow and human poses.
The inclusion of an optical flow stream increased hockey action classification accuracy by 10\%, demonstrated the complementary features presented in optical flow and human pose.
Gammulle et al.~\cite{gammulle2019PredictingFutureJointlyLearntModelActionAnticipation} approached the human action anticipation problem using a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) to simultaneously anticipate future actions and synthesise future scene embeddings.
This approach exhibited traits analogous to human neurological behavior in synthesising the future, and demonstrated that their model was better able to classify human actions with fewer frames.

While action prediction methods have progressed significantly, and methods involving flow have been previously explored, there is little work exploring more targeted uses of spatial patterns of flow at keypoints to capture anticipatory cues~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey}.


\section{Method}%
\label{sec:method}
We propose a novel feature extraction method designed to be easily integrated within existing state of the art skeleton-based action recognition models.
The feature extraction methodology, PoseOFF, is designed to sample information about early human visual motion cues.

By embedding these early motion cues as learnable features in skeleton-based action recognition architectures, we aim to show such features can support such state-of-the-art models achieving comparable accuracy with fewer observed frames.
Moreover, we seek to achieve higher action anticipation ability with minimal additional computational overheads, thus retaining the feasibility of the methods applied in real-time settings on standard computing hardware.

\subsection{PoseOFF}%
\label{subsec:PoseOFF}
% What is PoseOFF
Pose-guided Optical Flow Features, PoseOFF, is our proposed feature embedding for anticipatory human action prediction.
PoseOFF aims to capture localised spatio-temporal cues at the onset of human actions, anchored at each skeleton keypoint, which may be observable from the local deformation of optical flow fields.
The choice of sampling these areas in videos is well aligned with vision science literature, which has shown that human perception of other human movement is primed by human motion cues in these key areas in order to react and respond in a timely manner.
Given our targeted application areas require similar capabilities, it is of interest to explore how visual motion cues may be utilised in an artificial vision system.
An overview of the data extraction approach is depicted in~\autoref{fig:poseoff_extraction}.

% Two types of data extracted - First, pose
To obtain PoseOFF features for a video containing a human action, two types of data are extracted from videos; human skeleton keypoints (poses) and optical flow.
The input to the PoseOFF embedding extraction method is an RGB video of $T$ frames.
For each frame in the video, $I_t$ with frame number $t\in[1,T]$, we extract up to two human skeletons $M$, consisting of $V$ keypoints.
Each skeleton keypoint contains $C_{\text{pose}}=3$ channels.
Depending on the human pose extraction method, these skeleton keypoint channels either represent $(x,y,z)$ coordinates, where the z coordinate is an estimation of the distance that keypoint is from the camera, or $(x,y,\text{conf.})$, where $\text{conf.}$ is a value representing the pose estimation model's detection confidence level.

% Sample windows of optical flow
Based on the estimated locations of skeleton keypoints, square windows are samples from the optical flow vector field.
% Flow shapes
For adjacent frames $I_t$ and $I_{t+1}$ in the RGB video, optical flow is estimated to produce a total of $T-1$ optical flow vector fields, with $(u,v)$ vectors at each pixel location representing the motion that pixel took to reach it's current position in the latter frame.
Centred at every skeleton keypoint $(x_t, y_t)$, we compute and sample $N\times N$ windows of optical flow vectors, producing a window of shape $(N,N,2)$.
Optical flow vectors may be sampled at a pre-set dilation, where the dilation factor indicates the stride length in image space between adjacent vectors in the $N\times N$ grid.
Vectors are sampled at regular intervals set by a dilation factor to increase the receptive field.
Examples of dilated window sampling are presented in~\autoref{fig:dilations}.

Each optical flow feature window is flattened to produce $C_{\text{flow}}=2N^2$ channels.
These channels are appended to skeleton keypoint channels to produce a total of $C=C_{\text{pose}}+C_{\text{flow}}=2N^2+3$ channels for each skeleton keypoint.
The output of the PoseOFF embedding is a vector of shape:
\[ (T, M, V, C) \]

\subsection{Skeleton-based Action Recognition Models}
\label{subsec:models}
% How we modified the neural networks
To demonstrate the applicability and effectiveness of PoseOFF, we modify three state-of-the-art skeleton-keypoint action recognition models to incorporate learnable early visual motion cues.
The embedding layer of each model was modified to include a simple CNN designed to learn the features present in the windows of optical flow.
The CNN consisted of two convolutional layers separated by activation layers, pooling and a linear layer.
The output of these layers was concatenated with the original skeleton keypoints coordinates as additional channels in the skeleton graph.
A diagram of the PoseOFF embedding layer added to each model is presented in \autoref{fig:embedding_network_diagram}.

% Which models were tested?
The models tested using this methodology were InfoGCN++~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}, MS-G3D~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, and ST-GCN++~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition}. The models were trained using the hyperparametes defined by the models' authors, with settings as close to those provided in the respective papers.


% Trained on joint only stream
The PoseOFF extraction method utilises human skeleton \textit{joints} as locations in image space from which to extract windows of optical flow.
For this reason, the results presented in this paper are models trained and evaluated using the \textit{skeleton joint stream only}.

\subsubsectionmark{Observation Ratio}\label{subsubsec:obs_ratio}
To test each model's performance for the task of early action recognition, we employ temporal masking to simulate partial observation of the action.
Temporal masking hides the final frames so the model must perform action classification on only the early part of an input sequence, allowing one to measure how soon it can recognise what action is taking place.
Input sequences were temporally masked at specific ratios - \textit{observation ratios} - corresponding to proportions of the each model's temporal dimension.
\autoref{fig:poseoff_extraction} demonstrates a simplified example of a sequence with an observation ratio of 60\%; the model is shown the first 3 frames, and the last 2 frames are masked.
% That is, if the model expects an input sequence containing 100 frames, an sequence with an observation ratio of 10\% would only contain the first 10 frames of the original action sequence.
We used the temporal padding methods outlined in the skeleton-based action recognition papers; last frame duplication~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}, replaying action sequence~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, and zero padding~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition} to pad the partially observed sequences to the frame length for each model's input.


\subsection{Implementation}
\label{subsec:implementation}
% What algorithms did we use to calculate optical flow?
All models were implemented in Python using the PyTorch learning framework~\cite{paszkePyTorchImperativeStyleHighPerformanceDeepLearningLibrary}, using the code provided by their authors.
Optical flow was extracted using the PyTorch implementation of the RAFT algorithm~\cite{teed2020RAFTRecurrentAllPairsFieldTransformsOpticalFlow}.
The RAFT algorithm was selected on the basis that it provides very high accuracy optical flow estimation, and the model and weights come packaged with the PyTorch library for ease of implementation.
For the UCF-101 dataset, which contains only RGB data, human pose keypoints were extracted using the YOLO-POSE Large model~\cite{maji2022YOLOPoseEnhancingYOLOMultiPersonPoseEstimationUsingObjectKeypointSimilarityLoss}.

% Hyperparameter management?
The hyperparameters were chosen to match those defined by the model's authors for the NTU RGB+D 60 and NTU RGB+D 120 datasets.
For the UCF101 dataset, which did not have hyperparameters defined by the authors, we chose to mirror the settings used for the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset where possible.
Both the base model and PoseOFF embedded model were trained with identical hyperparameter settings.




\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}
The models were trained and evaluated on three different datsets; the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset~\cite{shahroudy2016NTURGB+DLargeScaleDataset3DHumanActivityAnalysis},NTU RGB+D 120 dataset~\cite{liu2020NTURGB+D120LargeScaleBenchmark3DHumanActivityUnderstanding} and the UCF101 dataset~\cite{soomro2012UCF101dataset101humanactionsclassesvideoswild}.

% About the NTU dataset
The NTU RGB+D 60 and NTU RGB+D 120 datasets were captured using three Microsoft Kinect v2 sensors and provide ground-truth 3D human skeleton data.
Each sample includes RGB videos, depth sequences, infrared frames, and 3D coordinates of 25 body joints.
The authors define standard train/test splits for each dataset, ensuring fair and consistent evaluation across methods.
Samples are annotated with one of 60 or 120 action classes, respectively.
Due to their widespread use in skeleton-based action recognition research ~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition, duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition, liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, these datasets were selected to establish baseline performance and to evaluate the impact of incorporating PoseOFF embeddings.

% About the UCF101 dataset
The UCF-101 dataset consists of unconstrained, real-world RGB videos depicting human actions.
As it does not provide skeleton or optical flow data, both were estimated using the methods described in Sec.~\ref{subsec:implementation}
The dataset includes three standard train/test splits.
UCF-101 was chosen to assess performance under more challenging, in-the-wild conditions, in contrast to the controlled capture settings of the NTU RGB+D datasets.

% Dilation factors between datasets
The RGB video data of the NTU RGB+D datasets has a resolution of $1920\times1080$ pixels, whereas the UCF101 dataset has a resolution of $320\times240$ pixels.
Windows of optical flow for the PoseOFF embedding were sampled with a dilation factor of 3 for the NTU RGB+D datasets and a dilation factor of 1 for the UCF101 datasets.
A higher dilation factor was chosen for the NTU RGB+D datasets given their higher video resolution.
See~\autoref{fig:dilations} an example of dilated window sampling.

\section{Experiments}%
\label{sec:experiments}

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
For the task of action classification, model performance was measured using the classification accuracy; that is, the percentage of samples that the models were able predict the correct class label of after observing the full action sequence.
For the task of action anticipation, both the classification accuracy at each given observation ratio and the Area Under the Curve (AUC) are reported.
The AUC is a measure of the model's performance across all evaluated observation ratios, and is calculated as:

\[ \text{AUC}=\frac{\sum{\text{Acc}}_{\text{obs}}}{\text{N}_{\text{obs}}}\]
where $\text{Acc}_{\text{obs}}$ is the accuracy at a given observation ratio, $\text{obs}$, and $\text{N}_{\text{obs}}$ is the number of evaluated observation ratios.


\subsection{Action Classification}%
\label{subsec:exp_action_classification}
% % How the results were obtained
To test the models' ability to perform action classification, all models - both the base model and a version of the model with the additional PoseOFF embedding layer, shown in~\autoref{fig:embedding_network_diagram} - were trained and tested on full sequences.
In the full sequence evaluation, no temporal masking was applied to the input sequence.
This test simulates an offline action recognition scenario.

In this table, we have highlighted the best performing model for each dataset and evaluation.
The models trained with the additional PoseOFF embeddings produce a higher classification accuracy across all models and all datasets.
Across all models, those trained with the additional PoseOFF embeddings showed an average increase in classification of 2.14\%, 6.84\% and 11.22\% for the NTU RGB+D 60, NTU RGB+D 120 and UCF101 datasets respectively.

The increase in action classification accuracy across all models and datasets is not unsurprising; the PoseOFF embeddings provide more information about the nature of the human motion taking place in the scene.

A higher action classification accuracy on this particular dataset reinforces notion that PoseOFF embeddings can provide increased salient information for real-world applications.

\subsection{Action Anticipation}%
\label{subsec:exp_action_anticipation}
% How we tested action anticipation
To test whether the early visual motion cues extracted though PoseOFF facilitate earlier prediction of actions at a similar accuracy level to baseline models, temporal masking was used to simulate partial observation of the action (i.e. viewing an action currently taking place).
The MS-G3D and ST-GCN++ models were trained using 100\% observation ratio and tested by viewing each video sequence at increments of 10\% observation ratios.
% To test the effectiveness of the learnable features present in PoseOFF data, we evaluated each model at different observation ratios of the input sequence.
% The models evaluated were trained on 100\% observation ratio and were evaluated at 10\% observation ratio intervals.
The InfoGCN++ model has temporal masking built in to the model and produces an action class prediction for every frame of the input sequence (64 frame input), so the observation ratio interval evaluations were not necessary for this model~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}.
These experiments were conducted using the Cross Subject (CS) evaluation of the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset, and the second evaluation defined by the authors of the UCF101 dataset.
The action anticipation results are presented in \autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ntu} and~\autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ucf}.

% How do we interpret these graphs?
The results are the average performance of the model's as they ``see'' more of each action sequence.
In \autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ntu} and \autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ucf}, each point along the blue and orange curves corresponds to the action classification accuracy (y-axis) of the baseline and PoseOFF-embedded models, respectively, for a given observation ratio (x-axis).
The gray dotted lines in these graphs indicates the highest performance of the base model, in order to compare it's performance with the PoseOFF embedded versions of the models.
The x-value at which the gray and blue lines intersect denotes the observation ratio at which the FlowPose embedded model achieves the same accuracy as the base model with full observation.
The observation ratio (x-value) of the point which the gray and blue lines intersect corresponds with the amount of the sequence that the PoseOFF embedded model needs in order to achieve the same action classification accuracy at the base model at 100\% observation ratio.


% NTU dataset results
The action anticipation results on the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset, shown in~\autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ntu}, show that all models are able to predict the action taking place with fewer frames.
For the ST-GCN++ and MS-G3D models, the PoseOFF embedded version of the model only needs to observe 80\% of the entire action sequence to achieve the same performance as the base model at 100\% observation ratio.
At lower observation ratios, the ST-GCN++ base model is able to predict the action more accurately than the PoseOFF embedded model.
For InfoGCN++, which was specifically designed for the task of action anticipation, the model utilising PoseOFF embeddeds needs to only see half of the input sequence to achieve the same performance as the base model at 100\% observation ratio.

% UCF action anticipation results
The action anticipation results on the UCF101 dataset, shown in~\autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio_ucf}, show again that PoseOFF embedded models require fewer frames to achieve the same action classification accuracy as the base model with a full observation of the sequence.
For all models, PoseOFF features enable the model to predict the action with the same accuracy as the base model after only seeing half of the full sequence.

We believe that the data produced from the PoseOFF extraction method can be easily implemented in any skeleton-based action recognition model to improve the model's action recognition and anticipatory prediction ability.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have demonstrated that the embedding of human early visual motion cues into established skeleton based human action recognition can increase overall action classification accuracy, and reduce number of frames required to achieve a competitive action classification accuracy.

The implementation of PoseOFF into existing skeleton-based action recognition models requires only a minor modification to the model's embedding layer.
By comparison to a two stream approach, using full optical flow frames as well as skeleton keypoints, PoseOFF presents a more data efficient method of extracting relevant human early visual motion cue information.

% Future work
In future work, we look to explore how the accuracy of the optical flow method choice impacts the benefits that PoseOFF provides.
If a faster, and perhaps less accurate method may be applied with little impact on demonstrated benefits, this would alleviate concerns that optical flow is too computationally expensive for real-time applications.
Additionally, future work may probe whether certain actions are more easily classified using these PoseOFF, which may offer further insights as to when it is most likely to offer benefits.
