\section{Introduction}
% Why is human early action recognition important?
Human action prediction is a central problem in computer vision, concerned with identifying actions from video data.
When predictions must be made rapidly within interactive systems, the task becomes action anticipation, which aims to accurately predict actions from as few observed frames as possible.
This capability is critical in applications such as human–robot teaming~\cite{yu2021MultisensorGestureInteractionSystemHumanrobotCooperation}, AR/VR~\cite{xu2025SeeingMyFuturePredictingSituatedInteractionBehaviorVirtualReality}, and video surveillance~\cite{ryoo2011HumanactivitypredictionEarlyrecognitionongoingactivitiesstreamingvideos}, where low-latency responses directly affect system performance.
Accordingly, action anticipation emphasizes prediction accuracy under strict constraints on speed and frame latency, enabling systems to pre-load resources or prime response actions for more natural human–machine interaction~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey}.

% early visual motion cues
Anticipatory action prediction is well established in the primate brain, where early visual motion cues play a key role in predicting and priming responses to observed actions~\cite{hagemann2006TrainingPerceptualSkillOrientingVisualAttention}.
These cues arise at early stages of visual processing through optical flow, where the perceived motion of light patterns on the retina—and are propagated to higher-level motor regions such as the premotor cortex~\cite{meichow2023Eyemovementsopticflowperception}.
Neuroscience studies further indicate that kinematic information from skeletal joint motion supports both action prediction and intent understanding\cite{gredeback2015EyeMovementsActionObservation}, motivating the integration of motion and pose cues in computer vision models of human action understanding.

% Human skeleton based action recognition
Human skeleton keypoints are 2D or 3D locations of major body joints extracted from images or videos that provide a compact and interpretable representation of body pose.
There keypoints are inherently static and lack local motion context.
While temporal tracking yields joint displacements, point-based representations fail to capture higher-order motion characteristics, such as acceleration, rotation, and local deformation, that are present in the surrounding optical flow field.
Consequently, skeleton-only models encode coarse motion patterns but miss fine-grained dynamics that are crucial for early action discrimination.

% Skeleton action recognition struggles to distinguish similar actions
This limitation is particularly evident for actions with similar gross body movements, such as distinguishing a handshake from a punch, keypoint-only models are unlikely to make accurate predictions until most of the action sequence has unfolded.
In contrast, keypoint-anchored visual motion cues (e.g., wrist acceleration, torso rotation or deformation) may provide early discriminative signals that support faster and more reliable action prediction.


% Action anticipation
Action classification and action anticipation are closely related but distinct tasks.
Action classification (also referred to as offline action recognition) assigns an action label after observing the complete action sequence, and therefore benefits from the full temporal context.
In contract, action anticipation aims to predict the action label from partial observations before the action has fully unfolded, requiring models to reason under temporal uncertainty and incomplete information~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey,chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}.

% Others have used optical flow for action recognition
Optical flow has previously been explored for action recognition~\cite{cai2019TemporalHockeyActionRecognitionPoseOpticalFlows,gammulle2019PredictingFutureJointlyLearntModelActionAnticipation,lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey,cai2020JOLOGCNMiningJointCenteredLightWeightInformationSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, primarily to support action classification.
However, prior work has not examined the use of localized, keypoint-anchored optical flow for action anticipation.
In contrast, we propose incorporating local optical flow regions centred at skeletal keypoints into skeleton-based action prediction architectures, augmenting keypoint trajectories with learned features derived from localized motion patterns.
By exploiting these cues at action onset, our approach enables earlier and more anticipatory predictions.

% Why optical flow and why PoseOFF?
To this end, we introduce \textbf{Pose-guided Optical Flow Features (PoseOFF)}, which involves \textit{targeted} sampling of optical flow windows around each human skeleton keypoint.
These features are integrated via a lightweight embedding layer within existing keypoint-based models, allowing them to capture nuanced local motion information beyond joint displacement alone.
We validate PoseOFF on three state-of-the-art skeleton-based action recognition models, demonstrating improved classification accuracy and significantly reduced prediction latency.


% Contributions
The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
  % Sampling flow around pose keypoints is a way to extract early visual motion cues
  \item PoseOFF, a novel feature embedding derived from optical to encode local spatial visual motion cues for anticipatory human action prediction from early visual motion cues.
  % Simple embedding layer
  \item A novel embedding layer that can be easily implemented in existing skeleton-based action recognition models to learn windows of optical flow.
  % Demonstration of increased accuracy
  \item The first demonstration that embedding early visual motion cues at pose-anchored locations can support reduced latency action predictions at comparable accuracy. We show this for three state-of-the-art skeleton-based action recognition models, using three established action classification datasets.
  % Demonstration of increased action anticipation
  \item Novel insights guiding the practical implementation of PoseOFF approach within real-time vision processing pipelines to support responsive and robust action anticipation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}%
\label{sec:related_work}
% Important recent methods
Action classification and anticipation are long-standing problems in computer vision, with growing interest in methods that support real-time and online prediction.
This work focuses on skeleton-based approaches, where 2D or 3D joint keypoints naturally lend themselves to graph-based learning.
State-of-the-art models such as ST-GCN++~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition} and MS-G3D~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition} employ Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) to classify actions from spatio-temporal skeleton sequences, but are primarily designed for offline action classification using complete observations.

% Action anticipation
To address action anticipation with skeleton data, Chi et al.~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition} introduced InfoGCN++, which incorporates future motion prediction to estimate unseen skeleton states, enabling action classification from fewer observed frames.
Despite such advances, skeleton-based methods largely ignore local visual dynamics present in RGB videos that may provide early discriminative cues.

% Visual motion in action prediction
Evidence from biological vision supports the importance of visual motion cues for anticipation.
In sports contexts, where tracking objects such as balls is difficult, elite athletes focus on information-dense body regions~\cite{mann2007PerceptualCognitiveExpertiseSportMetaAnalysis}.
Eye-tracking studies show that perception training targeting specific body cues improves anticipatory performance, such as predicting shot direction in football penalty scenarios~\cite{savelsbergh2010Anticipationpenaltykickingdirectioncanbeimproveddirectingattentionperceptuallearning}.

% Flow for human action recognition and action anticipation
In computer vision, such motion information can be captured via optical flow.
Prior work has demonstrated the complementary nature of pose and flow, including two-stream architectures that improve action classification accuracy~\cite{cai2019TemporalHockeyActionRecognitionPoseOpticalFlows} and generative approaches that synthesise future representations for action anticipation~\cite{gammulle2019PredictingFutureJointlyLearntModelActionAnticipation}.
Despite these advances, little work has explored targeted, keypoint-anchored optical flow to capture localized anticipatory motion cues in skeleton-based action prediction~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey}.


\section{Method}%
\label{sec:method}
We propose a novel feature extraction method designed to be easily integrated within existing state of the art skeleton-based action recognition models.
Our extraction methodology, PoseOFF, is designed to sample information about early visual motion cues.

By embedding early motion cues as learnable features in skeleton-based action recognition architectures, we aim to show such features can support such state-of-the-art models achieving comparable accuracy with fewer observed frames.
Moreover, we seek to achieve higher action anticipation ability with minimal additional computational overheads, thus retaining the feasibility of the methods applied in real-time settings on standard computing hardware.

\subsection{PoseOFF}%
\label{subsec:PoseOFF}
% What is PoseOFF
PoseOFF aims to capture localised spatio-temporal cues at the onset of human actions, anchored at each skeleton keypoint, which may be observable from the local deformation of optical flow fields.
% The choice of sampling these areas in videos is well aligned with vision science literature, which has shown that human perception of other's movement is primed by human motion cues in these key areas in order to react and respond in a timely manner.
% Given our targeted application areas require similar capabilities, it is of interest to explore how visual motion cues may be utilised in an artificial vision system.
An overview of the data extraction approach is depicted in~\autoref{fig:poseoff_extraction}.

% Two types of data extracted - First, pose
% To obtain PoseOFF features for a video containing a human action, two types of data are extracted from videos; human skeleton keypoints (poses) and optical flow.
The input to the PoseOFF embedding extraction method is an RGB video of $T$ frames.
For each frame in the video, $I_t$ with frame number $t\in[1,T]$, we extract up to two human skeletons $M$, consisting of $V$ keypoints.
Each skeleton keypoint contains $C_{\text{pose}}=3$ channels.
Depending on the human pose extraction method, these skeleton keypoint channels either represent $(x,y,z)$ coordinates, where the z coordinate is an estimation of the distance that keypoint is from the camera, or $(x,y,\alpha)$, where $\alpha$ is a value representing the pose estimation model's detection confidence level.

% Sample windows of optical flow
% Based on the estimated locations of skeleton keypoints, square windows are sampled from the optical flow vector field.
% Flow shapes
For adjacent frames $I_t$ and $I_{t+1}$ in the RGB video, optical flow is estimated, yielding $T-1$ optical flow vector fields, with $(u,v)$ vectors at each pixel location representing an estimation of the motion of the pixel from frame $I_t$ to $I_{t+1}$.
Centred at every skeleton keypoint $(x_t, y_t)$, an $N\times N$ window of optical flow vectors is sampled, producing an optical flow window of shape $(N,N,2)$.
Optical flow window sampling may be performed with a fixed dilation factor to increase the effective receptive field by spacing vectors uniformly across image space, as illustrated in~\autoref{fig:dilations}
% Optical flow vectors may be sampled at a pre-set dilation, where the dilation factor indicates the stride length in image space between adjacent vectors in the $N\times N$ grid.
% Vectors are sampled at regular intervals set by a dilation factor to increase the receptive field.
% Examples of dilated window sampling are presented in~\autoref{fig:dilations}.

Each optical flow feature window is flattened to produce $C_{\text{flow}}=2N^2$ channels.
These channels are appended to skeleton keypoint channels to produce a total of $C=C_{\text{pose}}+C_{\text{flow}}=2N^2+3$ channels for each skeleton keypoint.
The output of the PoseOFF extraction is a vector of shape:
\[ (T, M, V, C) \]

The blue cell in the center is the optical flow vector aligning with the pixel coordinates of the human pose keypoint.}

\subsection{Skeleton-based Action Recognition Models}
\label{subsec:models}
% How we modified the neural networks
To demonstrate the applicability and effectiveness of PoseOFF, we modify three state-of-the-art skeleton-keypoint action recognition models to incorporate learnable early visual motion cues.
The embedding layer of each model was modified to include a simple CNN designed to learn the features present in the windows of optical flow.
The CNN consisted of two convolutional layers separated by activation layers, pooling and a linear layer.
The output of these layers was concatenated with the original skeleton keypoints coordinates as additional channels in the skeleton graph.
A diagram of the PoseOFF embedding layer added to each model is presented in \autoref{fig:embedding_network_diagram}.

% Which models were tested?
The models evaluated were InfoGCN++~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}, MS-G3D~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, and ST-GCN++~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition}, trained using hyperparametes specified by the original authors and settings as close to those reported in the respective papers.


% Trained on joint only stream
The PoseOFF extraction method utilises human skeleton \textit{joints} as locations in image space from which to extract windows of optical flow.
For this reason, the results presented in this paper are models trained and evaluated using the \textit{skeleton joint stream only}.

\subsubsectionmark{Observation Ratio}\label{subsubsec:obs_ratio}
To test each model's performance for the task of early action recognition, we employ temporal masking to simulate partial observation of the action.
Temporal masking hides the final frames so the model must perform action classification on only the early part of an input sequence, allowing one to measure how soon it can recognise what action is taking place.
Input sequences were temporally masked at specific ratios - \textit{observation ratios} - corresponding to a proportion of the total frames accepted by the model as an input.
\autoref{fig:poseoff_extraction} shows a simplified example of a sequence with an observation ratio of 60\%; the model is shown the first 3 frames, and the last 2 frames are masked.
% That is, if the model expects an input sequence containing 100 frames, an sequence with an observation ratio of 10\% would only contain the first 10 frames of the original action sequence.
We used the temporal padding methods outlined in the skeleton-based action recognition papers; last frame duplication~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}, replaying action sequence~\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, and zero padding~\cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition} to pad the partially observed sequences to the frame length for each model's input.


\subsection{Implementation}
\label{subsec:implementation}
% What algorithms did we use to calculate optical flow?
All models were implemented in Python with the PyTorch learning framework~\cite{paszkePyTorchImperativeStyleHighPerformanceDeepLearningLibrary}, with the code provided by the authors.
Optical flow was extracted using the PyTorch implementation of the RAFT algorithm~\cite{teed2020RAFTRecurrentAllPairsFieldTransformsOpticalFlow}.
RAFT was chosen due to its high optical flow estimation accuracy and the availability of pretrained model weights within the PyTorch ecosystem.
For the UCF101 dataset, which contains only RGB data, human pose keypoints were extracted using the YOLO-POSE Large model~\cite{maji2022YOLOPoseEnhancingYOLOMultiPersonPoseEstimationUsingObjectKeypointSimilarityLoss}.

% Hyperparameter management?
The hyperparameters were chosen to match those defined by the model's authors for the NTU RGB+D 60 and NTU RGB+D 120 datasets.
For the UCF101 dataset, which did not have hyperparameters defined by the authors, we chose to mirror the settings used for the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset where possible.
Both the base model and PoseOFF embedded model were trained with identical hyperparameter settings.




\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}
The models were trained and evaluated on three different datsets; the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset~\cite{shahroudy2016NTURGB+DLargeScaleDataset3DHumanActivityAnalysis},NTU RGB+D 120 dataset~\cite{liu2020NTURGB+D120LargeScaleBenchmark3DHumanActivityUnderstanding} and the UCF101 dataset~\cite{soomro2012UCF101dataset101humanactionsclassesvideoswild}.

% About the NTU dataset
The NTU RGB+D 60 and NTU RGB+D 120 datasets were captured using three Microsoft Kinect v2 sensors and provide ground-truth 3D human skeleton data.
Each sample contains RGB videos, depth and infrared sequences, and 3D coordinates of 25 body joints.
Standard train/test splits defined by the authors ensure fair and consistent evaluation.
Samples are annotated with 60 and 120 action classes, respectively.
Owing to their widespread use in skeleton-based action recognition research ~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition, duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition, liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, these datasets were selected to establish baseline performance and to evaluate the impact of incorporating PoseOFF embeddings.

% About the UCF101 dataset
The UCF101 dataset consists of unconstrained, real-world RGB videos depicting human actions.
Skeleton keypoints and optical flow data were estimated using the methods described in Sec.~\ref{subsec:implementation}.
The dataset includes three standard train/test splits.
UCF101 was chosen to assess performance under more challenging, ``in-the-wild'' conditions, in contrast to the controlled capture settings of the NTU RGB+D datasets.

% Dilation factors between datasets
The RGB video data of the NTU RGB+D datasets has a resolution of $1920\times1080$ pixels, whereas the UCF101 dataset has a resolution of $320\times240$ pixels.
Windows of optical flow for the PoseOFF embedding were sampled with a dilation factor of 3 for the NTU RGB+D datasets and a dilation factor of 1 for the UCF101 datasets.
A higher dilation factor was chosen for the NTU RGB+D datasets given their higher video resolution.
See~\autoref{fig:dilations} an example of dilated window sampling.




\section{Experiments}%
\label{sec:experiments}

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
For action classification, performance is measured using classification accuracy, defined as the percentage of samples for which the correct class label is predicted after observing the full action sequence.
For action anticipation, we report classification accuracy at each observation ratio as well as the Area Under the Curve (AUC). The AUC summarizes performance across all evaluated observation ratios and is computed as:

\[ \text{AUC}=\frac{\sum{\text{Acc}}_{\text{obs}}}{\text{N}_{\text{obs}}}\]
where $\text{Acc}_{\text{obs}}$ denotes the accuracy at a given observation ratio, and $\text{N}_{\text{obs}}$ is the number of evaluated observation ratios.

No temporal masking was applied, simulating an offline action recognition setting.

\subsection{Action Anticipation}%
\label{subsec:exp_action_anticipation}
% How we tested action anticipation
To assess whether early visual motion cues extracted via PoseOFF enable earlier action prediction at comparable accuracy to baseline models, temporal masking was used to simulate partial observation of ongoing actions.
The MS-G3D and ST-GCN++ models were trained using full sequences (100\% observation ratio) and evaluated at 10\% observation ratio increments.

InfoGCN++ incorporates temporal masking natively and produces frame-level predictions over a fixed 64-frame input, making interval-based evaluation unnecessary\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}.
Action anticipation results are shown in~\autopageref{fig:res_obs_ratio}, using the Cross-Subject (CS) evaluation protocol on NTU RGB+D 60 (top) and the second evaluation protocol defined for UCF101 (bottom).


% How do we interpret these graphs?
The results are the average performance of the model's as they ``see'' more of each action sequence.
In \autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio}, each point along the blue and orange curves corresponds to the action classification accuracy (y-axis) of the baseline and PoseOFF-embedded models, respectively, for a given observation ratio (x-axis).
The gray dotted lines in these graphs indicates the highest performance of the base model, in order to compare it's performance with the PoseOFF embedded versions of the models.
The x-value at which the gray and blue lines intersect denotes the observation ratio at which the PoseOFF embedded model achieves the same accuracy as the base model with full observation.
% The observation ratio (x-value) of the point which the gray and blue lines intersect corresponds with the amount of the sequence that the PoseOFF embedded model needs in order to achieve the same action classification accuracy at the base model at 100\% observation ratio.

% NTU dataset results
The action anticipation results on the NTU RGB+D 60 dataset, shown in the first row of~\autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio}, show that all models are able to predict the action taking place with fewer frames.
For the ST-GCN++ and MS-G3D models, the PoseOFF embedded version of the model only needs to observe 80\% of the entire action sequence to achieve the same performance as the base model at 100\% observation ratio.
% At lower observation ratios, the ST-GCN++ base model is able to predict the action more accurately than the PoseOFF embedded model.
For InfoGCN++, which was specifically designed for the task of action anticipation, the PoseOFF embedded model needs to only see half of the input sequence to achieve the same performance as the base model at 100\% observation ratio.

% UCF action anticipation results
The action anticipation results on the UCF101 dataset, shown in~\autoref{fig:res_obs_ratio}, show again that PoseOFF embedded models require fewer frames to achieve the same action classification accuracy as the base model with a full observation of the sequence.
For all models, PoseOFF features enable the model to predict the action with the same accuracy as the base model after only seeing half of the full sequence.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
In this paper, we introduced PoseOFF, a novel feature extraction and embedding framework that integrates \textit{targeted} optical flow sampling around human skeleton keypoints to capture early motion cues for skeleton-based action recognition.
We have demonstrated that the embedding of early visual motion cues into established skeleton based human action recognition can increase overall action classification accuracy, and reduce number of frames required to achieve a competitive action classification accuracy.

The implementation of PoseOFF into existing skeleton-based action recognition models requires only a minor modification to the model's embedding layer.
In addition to being easy to implement and reducing latency, it comes with only a modest increase in computational overheads compared to baseline models (i.e., without PoseOFF), thus making it a feasible choice for real-time applications.
% By comparison to a two stream approach, using full optical flow frames as well as skeleton keypoints, PoseOFF presents a more data efficient method of extracting relevant human early visual motion cue information.

% Future work
Future work will consider the impact of alternative optical flow methods, in particular the potential use of faster, less accurate flow methods which may offer substantive performance benefits with minimal (if any) accuracy loss.
% If a faster, and perhaps less accurate method may be applied with little impact on demonstrated benefits, this would alleviate concerns that optical flow is too computationally expensive for real-time applications.
Additionally, future work will probe whether certain actions are more easily classified using these PoseOFF, which may offer further insights as to when it is most likely to offer benefits.
