\begin{abstract}
  This paper proposes a simple modification that can be made to existing human skeleton action recognition models that can improve action classification accuracy and human action anticipation capability.
  % What?
  We propose a method of extracting human early visual motion cues by sampling windows of optical flow surrounding skeleton keypoints in image space. We have titled this Pose-guided Optical Flow Feature methodology, PoseOFF.
  % So what?

  % Now what?

\section{Introduction}
Human early action recognition has applications in fields such as human-robot teaming~\cite{yu2021MultisensorGestureInteractionSystemHumanrobotCooperation}, pedestrian avoidance~\cite{rasouli2020PedestrianActionAnticipationusingContextualFeatureFusionStackedRNNs}, abnormal activity detection in surveillance~\cite{ryoo2011HumanactivitypredictionEarlyrecognitionongoingactivitiesstreamingvideos} and fall detection in aged care settings~\cite{DeeplearningvisionbasedfalldetectionsystemEnhancedopticaldynamicflow}.
The task of early action recognition focuses on both the accuracy and speed of action classification~\cite{lai2024HumanActionAnticipationSurvey}.
This is particularly useful in real-time applications, such as robotics, where robot response is time-critical.


% Skeleton-approaches BENEFITS
Human skeleton keypoints provide meaningful and succinct information about human actions.
Skeleton-based action recognition has grown as an area of interest in the last two decades.
The growing interest is partially due to the increase in availability of RGB-D sensors, giving rise to more skeleton-based action recognition datasets.
It is also partially due to the simplicity of the input data; human skeleton keypoints contain  \textit{only} information about the position and movement of humans and lack noisy background elements.

% Skeleton-approaches CHALLENGES
However, skeleton-only approaches only capture relations between limbs, and do not cope well with limb occlusion.
Skeleton action recognition datasets, such as NTU RGB+D~\cite{shahroudy2016NTURGB+DLargeScaleDataset3DHumanActivityAnalysis} and NTU RGB+D 120~\cite{liu2020NTURGB+D120LargeScaleBenchmark3DHumanActivityUnderstanding} use multiple RGB-D sensors to capture accurate human pose data from different viewpoints.
Skeleton-only learning approaches fail to capture joint features that may distinguish similar actions.

% RGB approaches CHALLENGES
RGB-only approaches to human action recognition must contend with large amounts of calculations needed to process video data.
Complex environments, such as outdoor settings or shifting camera perspectives, present challenges for RGB-only models in capturing robust human action features~\cite{wang2014CrossviewActionModelingLearningRecognition}.
RGB-only models designed to perform human early action recognition suffer when distinguishing intra-class differences and inter-class similarities.

% Why optical flow and why flowpose?
To address these challenged, we propose a method of RGB pixel motion information into existing skeleton-based action recognition models.
Our Pose-guided Optical Flow Feature extraction method, we have called ``PoseOFF'', involves the strategic sampling of windows of optical flow surrounding human skeleton keypoints.
Optical flow can provide more nuanced information that the simple displacement vectors of skeleton keypoints.
A simple embedding layer added to state-of-the-art skeleton-based action recognition algorithms shows an improvement in classification accuracy and action anticipation.


% Contributions
The main contributions of the proposed method are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
        % Sampling flow around pose keypoints is a way to extract early visual motion cues
  \item A feature extraction method for strategically sampling the optical flow field surrounding human pose keypoints.
        % Simple embedding layer
  \item A simple embedding layer that can be easily implemented in existing skeleton-based action recognition models to learn windows of optical flow.
        % Demonstration of increased accuracy and predictive ability
  \item An evaluation of three state-of-the-art skeleton-based action recognition models modified to learn additional optical flow features.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}%
\label{sec:related_work}
% GCN
In machine learning algorithm contexts, human skeleton keypoints depicted as graphs, with graph nodes and edges representing human joints and bones respectively.
This compact representation lends itself well to machine learning techniques based in graph theory, such as graph convolutional networks (GCNs) \cite{gori2005newmodellearninggraphdomains}.
% Graph convolution is a commonly used process in state-of-the-art skeleton human action recognition neural networks.
% Many of the state-of-the-art methods of learning human actions in sequences of skeleton keypoints is through graph convolution.

% ST-GCN++
A GCN proposed by Duan et al. \cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition}, ST-GCN++, achieved strong performance on skeleton-based action recognition datasets without the need for complicated attention mechanisms to model temporal dynamics. ST-GCN++ was designed to provide a strong baseline for future skeleton-based action recognition models.
% MS-G3D
Liu et al.
\cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition} designed MS-G3D, a skeleton-based action recognition model utilising spatial-temporal graph convolution modules to pass information across different graph scales.

% InfoGCN+
Existing human-action recognition models must capture the entire action sequence in order to make predictions, which may present latency issues for real-time applications such as human-robot interaction. Chi et al.~\cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition} incorporated a Neural Ordinary Differential Equations module into a skeleton-based action recognition model to model future skeleton states. The complete model, InfoGCN++, was able to classify human actions in skeleton datasets with fewer observed frames than existing skeleton-based action recognition models.

Multi-modal approaches to human action recognition area common. Cai et al. \cite{cai2019TemporalHockeyActionRecognitionPoseOpticalFlows} proposed a two-stream architecture for hockey action recognition that utilised both optical flow and human poses. The inclusion of an optical flow stream increased hockey action classification by 10\%.



\section{Methods}%
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{PoseOFF}%
\label{subsec:PoseOFF}
% What is PoseOFF
To extract human early visual motion cues from videos, we propose Pose-guided Optical Flow Features; ``PoseOFF''.
This is a general term for the method of sampling windows of optical flow surrounding human-pose keypoints.
We have chosen to sample optical flow surrounding human-pose keypoints as these are areas in videos of humans where motion relating to human actions take place (the human body).
Specifically, optical flow surrounding human pose keypoints was chosen as a way to embed human early visual motion cue information into existing skeleton action recognition models.

% Sampling optical flow windows with dilation
Based on our testing, a window size of $5\times5$ flow vectors demonstrated a good balance data efficiency and learnable features.
For videos with higher resolutions, we sample optical flow vectors with a dilated window.
Concretely, for the NTU RGB+D and NTU RGB+D 120 datasets, with a frame resolution of $1920\times1080$ pixels, we sample the optical flow vectors with a dilation of 3.
This provides a greater receptive field of optical flow vectors to be sampled without an increase in learnable data size.
The UCF-101 dataset has a resolution of $320\times240$ pixels, and we do not use dilated window optical flow sampling (dilation = 1).

% Why use flow and not just joint motion?
Optical flow provides richer information than joint displacement vectors. Where displacement indicate where a joint has come from, windows of optical flow can contain a more nuanced motion information about the moving pixels %ref.


% What algorithms did we use to calculate optical flow?
Optical flow was extracted using the Pytorch implementation of the RAFT algorithm~\cite{teed2020RAFTRecurrentAllPairsFieldTransformsOpticalFlow}.
The UCF-101 dataset is RGB only and does not include pre-computed human-pose keypoints.
We extract human pose keypoints using the YOLO-POSE Large model~\cite{maji2022YOLOPoseEnhancingYOLOMultiPersonPoseEstimationUsingObjectKeypointSimilarityLoss}.


\subsection{Models}
\label{subsec:models}
% How we modified the neural networks
We make a simple modification to three state-of-the-art skeleton-keypoint action recognition models to incorporate learnable early visual motion cues.
The embedding layer of each model was modified to include two convolutional layers for learning optical flow windows.
These learned features were then concatenated with the original skeleton keypoints coordinates as additional channels in the skeleton graph.

% Which models were tested?
The models tested using this methodology were InfoGCN++ \cite{chi2023InfoGCNLearningRepresentationPredictingFutureOnlineHumanSkeletonbasedActionRecognition}, MS-G3D \cite{liu2020DisentanglingUnifyingGraphConvolutionsSkeletonBasedActionRecognition}, and ST-GCN++ \cite{duan2022PYSKLGoodPracticesSkeletonActionRecognition}. The models were trained using the hyperparametes defined by the authors, with settings as close to those present in the respective papers.

% Trained on joint only stream
The PoseOFF extraction method utilises human skeleton \textit{joints} as locations in image space from which to extract windows of optical flow.
For this reason, the results presented in this paper are models trained and evaluated using the \textit{skeleton joint stream only}.
Each model was trained on full observations of input sequences and tested using masking of test sequences at $10\%$ observation ratios.
We used the temporal padding methods outlined in the respective papers to pad the partially observed sequences to the frame length for each model's input.

\section{Discussion}%
\label{sec:discussion}
% Discussion of results table...
The results table \ref{table:classification_accuay} demonstrates that the implementation of PoseOFF as additional learnable embeddings improves model performance on the datasets tested.
The optical flow features provide additional and useful learnable information.
This result is not particularly surprising given the fact that the flow vectors simply provide additional information to the model.

% The UCF101 dataset's results
The classification accuracy results of the UCF-101 dataset shows the most varied results between the models.
This dataset is RGB only dataset of human actions from videos ``in the wild''.
Human-pose keypoints were extracted manually using YOLO-POSE Large\cite{maji2022YOLOPoseEnhancingYOLOMultiPersonPoseEstimationUsingObjectKeypointSimilarityLoss}.
These manually extracted poses are less accuracy and less precise than poses included in the NTU RGB+D datasets.
Even with data extracted from videos . The models trained with data obtained using PoseOFF showed considerable improvement across the board.
This is an encouraging sign for real-world applications.

% \ref{fig:observation_accuracy} demonstrates that our model produces more accurate results. What this figure tells us is that our model, that utilises early visual motion cues, would only need to observe roughly 50\% of the input sequence to produce a classification accuracy comparable to that of the base model. This is more dramatic in the NTU RGB+D 120 dataset, which might indicate that our model is able to learn more complex features from a more complex dataset.
% \ref{fig:graph_attention}

% Discussion of action anticipation graph
The action anticipation results presented in~\ref{fig:obs_ratio_trained} are an indication that the PoseOFF can provide models without explicit future motion prediction features the ability to anticipate human actions.


% Why is this important?
The modifications made to the skeleton action recognition models were only in the first layers of the model, and did not significantly alter inner workings of the models.
These results demonstrate the effectiveness of data extracted using the PoseOFF method.
We believe that the data produced from the PoseOFF extraction method can be easily implemented in any skeleton-based action recognition model to improve the model's action recognition and anticipation ability.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have demonstrated that the embedding of human early visual motion cues into established skeleton based human action recognition can improve the classification accuracy of a model. We have also shown that .....

Established skeleton-based action recognition models yield greater classification accuracy results with the addition of human early visual motion cue information.

% Future work
In this work, PoseOFF was extracted with a fixed optical flow window sampling dilation. For the NTU-RGB+D and NTU-RGB+D 120 datasets, this flow sampling dilation was fixed to 3 pixels ($d=3$), and for the UCF-101 dataset, there was no dilation ($d=1$).
Future work could explore the effects of having a dynamic sampling dilation. Dilation could vary with quantities such as the apparent size of the human skeleton with respect to the frame size, or a skeleton keypoint's distance from the camera.
